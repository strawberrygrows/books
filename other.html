The Lessons, by Naomi Alderman
Like Brideshead Revisited and The Secret History happening at the same time. Nothing much new, but I enjoyed both of those and I enjoyed this too. And it's very readable, raced through it in three evenings.
High-Rise, by J.G. Ballard
This book is essentially Lord of the Flies for adults. Instead of a paradise island, it's a spectacularly expensive but poorly-designed tower block in Docklands area of London.<br><br>The central message of this book is that human civilisation is a construction and to an extent a performance, and therefore more fragile than we like to accept. The architecture, the isolation, and the savagery and tragedies that unfold in the High Rise, are all intimately connected. <br><br>There is some truth to this; evidence does suggest that people who live in impersonal tower blocks experience a whole host of issues from behavioural problems in children, lower reported life satisfaction and less empathy with neighbours. This book represents this taken to its logical extreme.<br><br>The constant self-analysis is a little obtrusive, characters sometimes feel like mouthpieces for the author (or the reader's GCSE English teacher). The author's habit of referring to individuals by their job titles is poignant in the face of the chaos but actually quite irritating especially when the POV character clearly does not know the person. Some scenes are profoundly uncomfortable, which is maybe 70% to do with the scene being described and 30% the deliberately awkward metaphors applied.<br><br>

The Prague Cemetery, by Umberto Eco
This is a marvellous book, a labyrinthine and only-moderately falsified history of European anti-semitism, conspiracies and forgeries. Surprisingly amusing in parts, as our joyless narrator obliviously presents opportunities for us to judge and mock his more lighthearted weird behaviour. He is very much a piece of shit from Page 1. And if you don't want to spend a couple of hundred pages reading violent anti-semitic tirades, I can hardly blame you.
The Name of the Rose, by Umberto Eco
'William of Baskerville' - the Holmes-like protagonist, and Adso of Melk, his German narrator, are two monks who come to an Italian abbey on a diplomatic mission for the Holy Roman Emperor. Upon arriving they find themselves tangled in heretical schemes and political machinations, as they try to solve a series of ghastly murders unfolding over a single week. At the centre is the library; a forbidden labyrinth which looms over the abbey and seems to be the key to solving these crimes. <br><br>This book is phenomenal: full of theological, literary, philosophical political ideas, and long discourses on these subjects scattered among the twin central plots of murder and diplomacy. Read it!
