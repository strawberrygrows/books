Books I read in 2025

Submission, by Michel Houellebecq
In the near future, a Muslim party sweeps to power in France. We see society transform (and the women disappear) through the eyes of François, a depressed literature professor specialising in Huysmans. Provocative, amusing, spectacularly French.

Cultish, by Amanda Montell
Interesting book on how cults, MLMs, and weirdly intense fitness instructors use language to attract and manipulate followers. Pretty superficial (and 3x the length it needs to be) but good fun.
Till We Have Faces, by CS Lewis
The myth of Cupid and Psyche, retold by Psyche's jealous elder sister. A glimpse into the terror, wonder, and monstrous ambiguity of faith, with an uncanny quality that's deeply unnerving. I sometimes felt like I was trespassing by reading it.
There Are Rivers in the Sky, by Elif Shafak
By the banks of the Thames and the Tigris, three lives are connected by water, memory, persecution, and the Epic of Gilgamesh. Beautifully written & lovingly researched – although I'd have liked more Gilgamesh and less dodgy science.
The Moonlight Market, by Joanne Harris
A fun modern fairytale to read on the plane but extremely repetitive/silly/shallow in places. Mostly feels like it's written for young children – I suppose it's aimed at adult women who mostly read YA/romantasy.
The Vegetarian, by Han Kang
When Yeong-hye, a dutiful wife, stops eating meat – that is only the beginning of the trouble. This was viscerally unpleasant and disturbing, but strangely fascinating. A volley in the South Korean gender wars. Point to the women.
Weight, by Jeanette Winterson
The story of Atlas holding up the world – and Heracles who takes up the burden briefly then tricks Atlas into taking it back. Interesting but shallow. Felt like reading a very long stream-of-consciousness blog post by someone quite self-involved.
The Virgin Suicides, by Jeffrey Eugenides
The neighbours boys chronicle their fascination with the five Lisbon sisters: watching the house, generating theories, and obsessively cataloguing stolen items. You can guess what happens to the girls. Read in one day; not sure why.
Letters to a Young Poet, by Rainer Maria Rilke
Almost every letter in this book opens with an apology for taking so long to write. Extremely charming and intense sequence. I slightly wish I'd read it as a teenager.
Sunrise on the Reaping, by Suzanne Collins
This was a Hunger Games book and therefore I loved it. The recurring themes are starting to become overfamiliar, and there's less tension because you know what happens – that's okay, I'll still keep reading that garbage.
Immortality, by Milan Kundera
Slower going than The Unbearable Lightness of Being (I almost stopped reading!) but in the end I enjoyed it just as much. Long and meandering, too postmodern for my tastes, but full of excellent paragraphs. The Goethe chapters are particularly good.
A Chess Story, by Stefan Zweig
It's a story about chess, can't say fairer than that. Hard to say anything else without spoiling it. I liked it.
Journey By Moonlight, by Antal Szerb
Finding himself restless and agitated on honeymoon in Italy, Mihály makes a series of unexpected decisions driven by fear, neuroticism, and nostalgia for his intense and unconventional teenage friendships. I read this in one day, totally hypnotised, and sat stunned and wounded for hours afterwards.
The Pendragon Legend, by Antal Szerb
A young Hungarian scholar visits Britain, where a chance meeting with a Welsh aristocrat draws him into a web of intrigue featuring long-dead alchemists, vengeful ghosts, and a secret dungeon full of axolotls. Extremely amusing.
Triumph and Disaster: Five Historical Miniatures, by Stefan Zweig
Another banger from Zweig. Scott's doomed expedition, the fall of Constantinople to the Ottomans, Lenin's journey to Russia in a sealed train – all excellent bedtime stories for aspiring Great Men of History.

The World of Yesterday, by Stefan Zweig
Heartbreaking elegy for pre-WWI Vienna. Zweig writes very movingly about what was lost in the world wars, and his own statelessness after fleeing Austria.
